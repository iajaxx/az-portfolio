OUR CORE
CREATIVE IDEA

It's bold.
That's why we chose it.
It has power and clarity
and optimism.

It's multifaceted, which makes it relevant to our full range of objectives and audiences, now and for years to come.

Part of why it's so powerful is that it takes what could be seen as a negative—that nothing's happening—and turns it on its head to make an attention-getting statement about the power peace and safety.

As you start to use this idea, it's critical to remember:
  • This isn't an idea about what didn't happen. And it's not an idea about nothing. It's an idea that celebrates everything that can.
  • It captures attention by taking a commonly held expression—that nothing happened—and twisting it as a positive.
  • It celebrates the result of our work—peace as a powerfully exciting foundation for creative, joyful, thriving cities—instead of lingering on fear.
  • It merges the power of human function with critical intelligence and shows our audiences as heroes and partners in peace and safety.
  • It ties directly to our Purpose by focusing on what the work we do makes possible in the moments that matter to everyone.

THE CORE IDEA IS:

THE SINGLE DRIVING CONCEPT FOR OUR COMMUNICATIONS AT EVERY LEVEL

It brings our Purpose to life through the lens of public safety, galvanizing our customers with vivid portrayals of safer cities as the foundation for a more peaceful, joyful, creative, entrepreneurial world.

It's also the DNA for all our messaging—from how we write sales and presentation copy to how we design our trade booth experiences.

THE CORE IDEA IS NOT:

A TAGLINE.

A tagline is a single line created for public use, usually displayed in conjunction with a logo. It's like the core idea in that it represents an organization's reason for being or point of view; it's unlike our core idea in that it never changes. No one at Nike, for instance, would ever consider something like “Go on” instead of “Just do it” as an appropriate pairing with their logo on a billboard.

Motorola Solutions does not have a tagline, and the core idea should never be paired with our logo.

A COMMERCIAL CAMPAIGN.

Campaign lines are also external and designed to last for a limited time—often just 3–6 months—with a limited purpose, usually to get people excited about a new product. Our big idea is absolutely intended to get people excited, but because it captures the essence of the new Motorola Solutions, we plan on it being in use for quite a long time, and having the ability to extend for years to come.

A HEADLINE.

We want the big idea to stay big. Headlines in brochures, web pages, banner ads, and/or subject lines in emails are great places to evoke the spirit of our big idea, but not to repeat it over and over.

USING CURRENT NEWS TO TURN THAT INFORMATION INTO A COMPELLING PR STORY
FIGHTING FIRE WITH RADIO

How the APX P25 Personnel Accountability Application saves those who save us

When a 6:30am fire swept through a four-unit condominium in Alsip, residents had already been evacuated. But the danger was far from over. Firefighters battled a building-threatening blaze in between floors and under the roof, risking their lives to save residents' homes as waterlogged trusses sagged beneath them and roaring flames made hearing nearly impossible. Outside, incident commanders used the Motorola Solutions APX P25 Personnel Accountability Application's single, simple interface to monitor who was where, whose oxygen tanks were running low—and then, with the touch of a button, to let everyone know when it was time to get out.

Because the accountability solution allows for simultaneous alert and acknowledgement of the evacuation, there was no need to use an air horn apparatus or wait for a manual personnel check. Firefighters hit their PTT buttons and exited faster than ever before—allowing them to fight fires longer, better, faster and more safely.

Note: This story is completely accurate, as all our stories must be. Our products, services and solutions speak for themselves, and the world of public safety and first response is inherently dramatic. As long as we keep an eye out for the right moments and use our core creative idea to talk about them in terms of what's at stake for everyone involved, even our simplest stories can be exciting—and we'll be strengthening our brand.

What we did:

Started with an attention-getting, concise headline

Used the subhead to depict firefighters as the real heroes of the story

Showcased the application in the context of a dramatic, inherently human story

Let readers know what was at stake for everyone involved

Painted a vivid picture of being in heart of the fire and of the challenge of getting out, and added drama with the almost depleted oxygen tanks

Talked about the application's features and benefits in terms of the ultimate benefits they provide

VIDEOS

When used with discipline, video can be visceral and exciting—a perfect medium for showcasing both the heroism of those who put themselves on the line to keep cities safe and the critical, moment-by-moment intelligence we support them with.
Videos are also one of the most easily sharable brand assets we have, so it's especially important that they show us at our best. It's essential to follow these principles when you're adding videos to our YouTube page.
CONTENT ORDER
  • Brand messages should always be at the top of the page. These are high-level stories that talk directly about the core idea in one of its three main forms—everything to see here, when nothing happens everything else can, and nothing (bad) happened here—by focusing on the benefits we make possible: safe, peaceful, thriving communities around the world.
  • Case studies are the next level down. These are individual stories of specific moments in which our technologies and services are transforming safety. While they're focused on individual services, solutions or products, they always tie those things to the larger benefits.
  • Thought leadership is the one category in which Motorola Solutions takes center stage, but even then the content should always focus on how our views and capabilities are leading the way to safer cities and thriving communities. These videos make the greatest impact when they're focused on news “pegs”— current events that relate directly to the work we do.
  • DIY/event videos. Done with restraint, these videos can be great for capturing the live spirit of an event and for showing products and services in action. But they should never be featured on the Motorola YouTube home page. They don't rise to the level of brand videos, and as a result they can diminish the strength of our core idea, which diminishes the power of our brand.

VIDEOS

CONTENT STRUCTURE

All video content should follow, in one form or another, classic story structure: we meet a hero (always the customer or those the customer serves), see them confronted with a challenge, and then see Motorola Solutions at work enabling them to overcome that challenge.

ASK YOURSELF THESE QUESTIONS TO GET THE MATERIAL YOU NEED:
  • What's the customer's key challenge?
  • What's at stake for them if they fail to rise to that challenge? What's at stake for the people they serve—their own customers, or the general populace?
  • What's a specific example of an obstacle they had to overcome to face that challenge—a limited amount of time? Few resources? Remote locations? Systemic skills gaps?
  • What couldn't the customer do before we partnered with them that they can do now? What's changed?
  • How does overcoming those challenges make the customer's city safer? Help its communities thrive?

THE BOOTH INTERIOR
